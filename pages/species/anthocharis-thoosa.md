<p><span><span><strong>Photo Life History:</strong>&nbsp; <a href="../anthocharis-sara-immatures/ianthocharis-thoosa-thoosai/index.html"><em>Anthocharis thoosa</em></a></span></span></p>
<p><span><strong>Habitat:</strong></span>&nbsp; <a href="../habitat/mountain-canyons/index.html">Mountain Canyons</a></p>
<p><span><strong>Host Plants:</strong></span>&nbsp; <span style="text-decoration: none;"><em><a href="../descurainia-pinnata/index.html">Descurainia pinnata</a></em>; <em><a href="../arabis-perennans/index.html">Arabis perennans</a></em>; <em>Arabis holboellii</em>;<em> <a href="../isatis-tinctoria/index.html">Isatis tinctoria</a></em></span></p>
<p><strong>Suitable Lab Host Plants</strong>: Any <em>Arabis</em> species; <em><a href="../isatis-tinctoria/index.html">Isatis tinctoria</a></em></p>
<p><strong>Caring for Live Female Butterflies:</strong>&nbsp; <a href="../caring-for-live-females.html">Feed females regularly</a>; Do NOT place live females in glassine envelopes for ANY period of time.&nbsp; They stress easily and can die.&nbsp; Also, they can lose legs when placed in a glassine envelope.</p>
<p><strong>Methods of Female Oviposition:</strong>&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="../female-oviposition2/portable-cages/index.html">Portable Cages</a>; <a href="../female-oviposition2/open-screen-cages/index.html">Open Screen Cages</a></p>
<p><strong>How to Find&nbsp;Eggs:</strong> When looking on <em>Arabis, </em><a href="../finding-eggs-in-the-field/eggs-on-flower-stems/index.html">Look on Flower Stems</a>; When looking on <em>Descurainia pinnata</em>, <a href="file:///C:/butterfl/raisingbutterflies.org/html%20pages/Species%20Accounts/finding-eggs-in-the-field/eggs-on-upper-half-of-plant/">Look on Upper Half of Plant</a>; <a href="../isolated-host-plants/index.html">Isolated Host Plants</a> (In other words, if you encounter one Juniper tree with 50 <em>D. pinnata</em> plants growing under it; vs. another Juniper tree with 3 <em>D. pinnata</em> plants growing under it, look on the three plants first.)</p>
<p><strong>How to Hatch Eggs:</strong>&nbsp; <a style="mozoutline: 0;" href="../hatching-eggs/separate-eggs-individually/index.html">Separate eggs&nbsp;individually</a>; <a href="http://indroid.squarespace.com/hatching-eggs/keep-egg-on-original-leaf/">Keep egg on original leaf</a></p>
<p><strong>How to Find Caterpillars in the Field:</strong>&nbsp; It is abundantly easier to find eggs and/or to get eggs out of live females.&nbsp; Try to show up about 7 - 10 days after the males have started flying.&nbsp; It can be difficult to locate late instar larvae, (many will have been taken out by predation.)&nbsp; Look for <a href="../picture/p_beckeri_s_pinnata.jpg%3FpictureId=3419567&amp;asThumbnail=true">Caterpillar Strip Patterns</a>.&nbsp; Most <em>A. thoosa</em> mustards are also used commonly by <em>Pontia sisymbri</em>, <em>Euchloe hyantis</em>, <em>Euchloe ausonides</em>, and sometimes <em>Pontia protodice</em>.</p>
<p><strong>Caterpillar setups:</strong>&nbsp; <a href="../caterpillar-setups/open-terrariums/index.html">Open terrariums</a>; <a href="../caterpillar-setups/open-bucket/index.html">Open Bucket</a></p>
<p><strong>Overwintering Stage:</strong>&nbsp; Pupa.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Overwintering Strategies:</strong> <a href="../overwintering-techniques/your-own-backyard/index.html">Your Own Backyard</a>; <a href="../overwintering-techniques/refrigerator/index.html">Refrigerator</a></p>
<p><strong>Larva to Pupa:</strong>&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="http://www.raisingbutterflies.org/picture/a_thoosa_change.jpg?pictureId=3636535&amp;asThumbnail=true">Larva Changes Color</a> (goes from medium green to dark green)</p>
<p><strong>Emergence:</strong>&nbsp; <a href="../emerging-butterflies/index.html">Emergence Container</a></p>
<p><strong>Number of Broods per Year: </strong>1 brood; excepting some populations of <em>inghami</em> that occasionally have a second generation.</p>
<p><strong>Avoiding Diapause Techniques: </strong>&nbsp;Not effective for univoltine populations.&nbsp; However, if you&nbsp;expose ssp. <em>inghami </em>late instar larvae and/or pupae to rain water, that may encourage same year emergence in populations that have been known to be bivoltine during extraordinarily wet springs.</p>
<p><strong>Disease Prevention:</strong>&nbsp; Change out host plant and remove frass every three to four days.</p>
<p><strong>Field Notes:</strong>&nbsp; Due to field research (in press), subspecies <em>thoosa, inghami, </em>and<em> colorado </em>are applied to species '<em>thoosa</em>'.&nbsp; Populations of <em>Anthocharis sara</em> feed on <em>Brassica nigra</em> in California whereas Utah populations of <em>Anthocharis thoosa</em> accept <em>Brassica nigra</em> for a few instars and die.</p>
